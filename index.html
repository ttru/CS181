AI on the Battlefield

What place, if any, do fully autonomous lethal weapons have on the battlefield? What can and should be done to minimize the risks they present?

For centuries, humans have used technical breakthroughs as means of improving their odds in warfare. From wooden spears to .50 caliber machine guns, the way we wage war has already seen great changes; but it is the rise of computers may completely transform the battlefield. The Information Age quickly digitalized our aircraft, ships, weapons, and communications, but the advancement of artificial intelligence may change the way we use these tools all together.

As the idea of artificial intelligence (AI) becomes more of a reality than science fiction, governments are beginning to research, develop, and utilize AI in war and on the battlefield. In particular, the world has been discussing the autonomy of weapon systems. There are three degrees of autonomy when we talk about autonomous weapon systems: in-the-loop, on-the-loop, out-of-the-loop.

  • In-The-Loop
  • On-The-Loop
  • Out-Of-The-Loop

In-the-loop autonomous weapon systems are those that have some level of autonomy, but any critical decisions are made remotely by a human.

MQ-9 Reaper

Unmanned Aerial Vehicles, or drones, like this MQ-9 Reaper have single handedly changed the way governments collect intelligence and deploy airstrikes. They have been in use since the creation of the Gnat, renamed the Predator, in 1995, and are now an integral tool used in most missions. Today, the US Air Force has more pilots training to fly UAVs than manned aircraft.
